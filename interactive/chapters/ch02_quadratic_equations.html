Quadratic Equations

Master the art of solving equations of degree 2 — by factorization, completing the square, and the powerful quadratic formula.

🎯 The Hook

The Swimming Pool Problem

Saleem wants to build a swimming pool surrounded by a sidewalk of uniform width. The total outer dimensions (pool + sidewalk) are 16 m by 20 m. The pool itself has an area of 192 m².

How wide should the sidewalk be?

Setting up the equation:

Pool length $= 20 - 2x$, Pool width $= 16 - 2x$

$$\text{Area} = (20 - 2x)(16 - 2x) = 192$$

$$4x^2 - 72x + 320 = 192$$

$$4x^2 - 72x + 128 = 0$$

This is a quadratic equation — an equation with $x^2$ as the highest power. To find the sidewalk width, we need to solve it!

📜 The Origins

The Art of al-Jabr

The quadratic equation you just encountered has a history spanning nearly two thousand years. Its solutions were forged across civilizations — from ancient Alexandria to the golden age of Baghdad.

250 AD
Diophantus of Alexandria solves specific quadratic equations in his landmark work Arithmetica, but lacks general methods — each problem is treated as a unique puzzle.
628 AD
Brahmagupta in India gives the first general formula for solving quadratic equations, including revolutionary rules for negative numbers. His work traveled along trade routes to Baghdad and directly influenced later scholars.
820 AD
Al-Khwarizmi writes Al-Jabr wal-Muqabala in Baghdad — the founding text of algebra. He systematically solves quadratic equations using geometric methods, literally completing the square with actual squares drawn on paper! His name gives us the word “algorithm,” and his book title gives us “algebra.” Pakistani students are direct heirs to this extraordinary mathematical tradition.
1545
Gerolamo Cardano in Italy extends the ideas to cubic equations in his masterwork Ars Magna, building on the algebraic foundations laid by Al-Khwarizmi.
1637
René Descartes in France introduces modern algebraic notation, giving the quadratic formula the elegant symbolic form $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ that we use today.

Your Mathematical Heritage

When you “complete the square” in this chapter, you are performing the exact same technique Al-Khwarizmi demonstrated over 1,200 years ago — except he drew actual geometric squares to show why it works! The House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) in Baghdad, where he worked, was the world’s greatest center of learning, attracting scholars from Persia, India, and beyond. This is your mathematical heritage: algebra was born in the Islamic world, and every quadratic equation you solve connects you to that golden tradition.

1 Standard Form of a Quadratic Equation ▼

A quadratic equation is an equation that can be written in the form:

$$ax^2 + bx + c = 0, \quad a \neq 0$$

where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are real numbers and $a \neq 0$. This is called the standard form.

  • The degree of the equation is 2 (highest power of the variable).
  • $a$ is the coefficient of $x^2$, $b$ is the coefficient of $x$, and $c$ is the constant term.
  • A quadratic equation in one variable has at most two roots.

Examples: Converting to Standard Form

(i) $(x+2)(x-3) = 5$

$x^2 - x - 6 = 5 \implies x^2 - x - 11 = 0$

(ii) $(3x-1)^2 = 4$

$9x^2 - 6x + 1 - 4 = 0 \implies 9x^2 - 6x - 3 = 0 \implies 3x^2 - 2x - 1 = 0$

2 Method 1: Factorization ▼
Factorization

This method uses the Zero Product Property:

If $ab = 0$, then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$.

Procedure:

  1. Write the equation in standard form.
  2. Factorize the left-hand side into two linear factors.
  3. Apply zero product property to get two linear equations.
  4. Solve each linear equation to find the roots.

Example: Solve $x^2 - 18x + 32 = 0$

Split the middle term
$x^2 - 16x - 2x + 32 = 0$
Group and factor
$x(x - 16) - 2(x - 16) = 0$
Common factor
$(x - 2)(x - 16) = 0$
Zero product property
$x - 2 = 0$ or $x - 16 = 0$
Solution
$x = 2$ or $x = 16 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S.S. = \{2, 16\}$

Example: Solve $x^2 - 2x - 15 = 0$

$x^2 - 5x + 3x - 15 = 0$
$(x - 5)(x + 3) = 0$
$x = 5$ or $x = -3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S.S. = \{-3, 5\}$
3 Method 2: Completing the Square ▼
Completing the Square

When factorization is not straightforward, we can complete the square to create a perfect square trinomial on one side.

Procedure:

  1. Write the equation in standard form.
  2. Move the constant term to the right side.
  3. If the coefficient of $x^2$ is not 1, divide the entire equation by it.
  4. Add the square of half the coefficient of $x$ to both sides.
  5. Factor the left side as a perfect square.
  6. Take the square root of both sides and solve.
For $x^2 + bx$: add $\left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$ to get a perfect square $\left(x + \dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$

Example: Solve $3x^2 - 11x - 4 = 0$

Move constant to the right
$3x^2 - 11x = 4$
Divide by 3
$x^2 - \dfrac{11}{3}x = \dfrac{4}{3}$
Half of $-\frac{11}{3}$ is $-\frac{11}{6}$. Square it: $\frac{121}{36}$. Add to both sides.
$x^2 - \dfrac{11}{3}x + \dfrac{121}{36} = \dfrac{4}{3} + \dfrac{121}{36}$
Factor left side, simplify right side
$\left(x - \dfrac{11}{6}\right)^2 = \dfrac{48 + 121}{36} = \dfrac{169}{36}$
Take square root
$x - \dfrac{11}{6} = \pm\dfrac{13}{6}$
Solve
$x = \dfrac{11 + 13}{6} = 4 \quad$ or $\quad x = \dfrac{11 - 13}{6} = -\dfrac{1}{3}$
$S.S. = \left\{-\dfrac{1}{3},\; 4\right\}$

Example: Solve $3x^2 + 6x - 9 = 0$

$x^2 + 2x = 3 \quad$ (dividing by 3)
$x^2 + 2x + 1 = 3 + 1 \quad$ (adding $1$ to both sides)
$(x + 1)^2 = 4$
$x + 1 = \pm 2$
$x = 1$ or $x = -3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S.S. = \{-3, 1\}$
4 Method 3: The Quadratic Formula ▼
Quadratic Formula

The most powerful method — works for any quadratic equation. It is derived by applying the completing-the-square method to the general form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.

Derivation:

Start with standard form
$ax^2 + bx + c = 0$
Move $c$ to the right, divide by $a$
$x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x = -\dfrac{c}{a}$
Add $\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ to both sides
$x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} = \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} - \dfrac{c}{a}$
Factor left side, simplify right side
$\left(x + \dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \dfrac{b^2 - 4ac}{4a^2}$
Take square root of both sides
$x + \dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm\dfrac{\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$
Solve for $x$
$x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$
The Quadratic Formula $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

Example: Solve $2x^2 + 7x - 4 = 0$

Here $a = 2$, $b = 7$, $c = -4$.

$x = \dfrac{-7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 4(2)(-4)}}{2(2)} = \dfrac{-7 \pm \sqrt{49 + 32}}{4}$
$x = \dfrac{-7 \pm \sqrt{81}}{4} = \dfrac{-7 \pm 9}{4}$
$x = \dfrac{-7 + 9}{4} = \dfrac{1}{2} \quad$ or $\quad x = \dfrac{-7 - 9}{4} = -4$
$S.S. = \left\{-4,\; \dfrac{1}{2}\right\}$
Procedure Summary:
  1. Write the equation in standard form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  2. Identify $a$, $b$, and $c$.
  3. Substitute into $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$.
  4. Simplify and write the solution set.

5 Discriminant & Nature of Roots ▼
Discriminant

In the quadratic formula, the expression under the square root is called the discriminant:

$$D = b^2 - 4ac$$

The discriminant tells us the nature of the roots without actually solving the equation.

Value of $D = b^2 - 4ac$ Nature of Roots Example
$D > 0$ (perfect square) Real, Rational, Unequal $x^2 - 3x - 28 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; D = 121$
$D > 0$ (not perfect square) Real, Irrational, Unequal $3t^2 - 6t + 2 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; D = 12$
$D = 0$ Real, Rational, Equal $x^2 - 8x + 16 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; D = 0$
$D < 0$ Imaginary / Complex $3y^2 - 5y + 9 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; D = -83$

Example: Find $m$ if $x^2 - 3x + m = 0$ has equal roots.

For equal roots, $D = 0$:

$b^2 - 4ac = 0 \implies (-3)^2 - 4(1)(m) = 0 \implies 9 - 4m = 0 \implies m = \dfrac{9}{4}$

Remember: If $D < 0$, the roots contain $\sqrt{\text{negative number}}$, which gives imaginary numbers. These are not real roots!

6 Sum & Product of Roots ▼
Roots Relationship

If $S_1$ and $S_2$ are the roots of $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, then:

Sum of Roots: $\quad S_1 + S_2 = -\dfrac{b}{a} = -\dfrac{\text{coefficient of } x}{\text{coefficient of } x^2}$

Product of Roots: $\quad S_1 \cdot S_2 = \dfrac{c}{a} = \dfrac{\text{constant term}}{\text{coefficient of } x^2}$

Example: For $3x^2 - 11x - 4 = 0$, find the sum and product of roots.

Here $a = 3$, $b = -11$, $c = -4$.

Sum $= -\dfrac{b}{a} = -\dfrac{-11}{3} = \dfrac{11}{3}$

Product $= \dfrac{c}{a} = \dfrac{-4}{3}$

Example: If $S_1, S_2$ are roots of $x^2 - 8x + 20 = 0$, find $S_1^2 + S_2^2$.

$S_1 + S_2 = 8, \quad S_1 \cdot S_2 = 20$

$S_1^2 + S_2^2 = (S_1 + S_2)^2 - 2S_1 S_2 = 64 - 40 = 24$

7 Forming Equations from Given Roots ▼

If $S_1$ and $S_2$ are the given roots, let:

$S = S_1 + S_2$ (sum of roots) and $P = S_1 \cdot S_2$ (product of roots)

Formation Formula $$x^2 - Sx + P = 0$$

Example 1: Form a quadratic equation whose roots are 5 and 6.

$S = 5 + 6 = 11, \quad P = 5 \times 6 = 30$

Equation: $x^2 - 11x + 30 = 0$

Example 2: Form a quadratic equation whose roots are $3 + 2i$ and $3 - 2i$.

$S = (3+2i) + (3-2i) = 6$

$P = (3+2i)(3-2i) = 9 - 4i^2 = 9 + 4 = 13$

Equation: $x^2 - 6x + 13 = 0$

Example 3: If $S_1, S_2$ are roots of $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, form the equation whose roots are $2S_1$ and $2S_2$.

New Sum $= 2S_1 + 2S_2 = 2(S_1+S_2) = 2\left(-\dfrac{b}{a}\right) = -\dfrac{2b}{a}$

New Product $= (2S_1)(2S_2) = 4S_1S_2 = \dfrac{4c}{a}$

Equation: $x^2 + \dfrac{2b}{a}x + \dfrac{4c}{a} = 0 \implies ax^2 + 2bx + 4c = 0$

Chapter 2 — Key Takeaways

  • $ax^2 + bx + c = 0, \; a \neq 0$ is the standard form of a quadratic equation.
  • Three solving methods: Factorization, Completing the Square, and the Quadratic Formula.
  • The Quadratic Formula: $\;x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$
  • The Discriminant $D = b^2 - 4ac$ determines the nature of roots.
  • Sum of roots $= -\dfrac{b}{a}$,   Product of roots $= \dfrac{c}{a}$
  • To form an equation from roots: $x^2 - Sx + P = 0$ where $S$ = sum, $P$ = product.
