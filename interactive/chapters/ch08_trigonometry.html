Quest 08

Application of Trigonometry

Measure the unmeasurable. Find heights of buildings, distances across rivers, and areas of land — all with angles and triangles.

🎭 The Hook

The Minar-e-Pakistan Problem

You need to find the height of Minar-e-Pakistan but can't climb it. All you have is a measuring tape and a protractor. How do you measure something you can't reach?

Stand some distance from the base. Measure the angle between the ground and the top of the tower (angle of elevation). Measure how far you are from the base. With just these two measurements and the power of trigonometry, you can calculate the exact height.

But what if the triangle isn't right-angled? Real-world triangles rarely have a 90-degree angle. For oblique triangles, we need more powerful tools: the Law of Cosines and the Law of Sines. These unlock the ability to solve any triangle.
🎯 The Challenge

Can You Solve This?

Before learning the laws, try this with intuition:

Challenge: Two fire towers A and B are located 10 miles apart. Rangers at tower A spot a fire at an angle of 42 degrees from line AB. Rangers at tower B spot the same fire at 64 degrees from line AB.

  • Can you figure out how far the fire is from tower A?
  • What information would you need?

This is a classic ASA triangle problem. By the end of this chapter, you'll solve it easily with the Law of Sines.

💡 Discovery Zone

Concepts Unlocked

1 Law of Cosines ▼

For any triangle $ABC$ with sides $a$, $b$, $c$ opposite to angles $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$:

$$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos\alpha$$ $$b^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos\beta$$ $$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos\gamma$$
When to use:
  • SAS — Two sides and the included angle are given
  • SSS — All three sides are given (find angles)

To find angles when three sides are known:

$$\cos\alpha = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}$$
Special case: When $\gamma = 90°$, $\cos 90° = 0$, so $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$, which is the Pythagorean Theorem! The Law of Cosines is a generalization of Pythagoras.

Example

Solve triangle $ABC$ when $a = 12$ cm, $b = 7$ cm, $\gamma = 59°30'$.

$c^2 = 12^2 + 7^2 - 2(12)(7)\cos 59°30' = 144 + 49 - 168 \times 0.506 = 108$

$c = \sqrt{108} \approx 10.4$ cm

$\cos\alpha = \frac{7^2 + 10.4^2 - 12^2}{2(7)(10.4)} = \frac{49 + 108.16 - 144}{145.6} \approx 0.0904$

$\alpha \approx 84°49'$, and $\beta = 180° - 84°49' - 59°30' = 35°41'$

2 Law of Sines ▼

For any triangle $ABC$:

$$\frac{a}{\sin\alpha} = \frac{b}{\sin\beta} = \frac{c}{\sin\gamma}$$
When to use:
  • AAS / ASA — Two angles and one side
  • SSA — Two sides and an angle not included (ambiguous case)
The Ambiguous Case (SSA): When given two sides and an angle opposite one of them, there may be 0, 1, or 2 possible triangles. Always check whether $\sin\beta \leq 1$ and whether $\alpha + \beta < 180°$.

Example

Solve triangle $ABC$: $a = 25$ cm, $\alpha = 66°51'$, $\gamma = 44°12'$.

$\beta = 180° - 66°51' - 44°12' = 68°57'$

$c = \frac{25 \times \sin 44°12'}{\sin 66°51'} \approx 18.96$ cm

$b = \frac{25 \times \sin 68°57'}{\sin 66°51'} \approx 25.37$ cm

3 Half-Angle Formulas ▼

When three sides $a, b, c$ of a triangle are known, let the semi-perimeter be $S = \frac{a+b+c}{2}$. Then:

Cosines of half angles:

$$\cos\frac{\alpha}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{S(S-a)}{bc}}, \quad \cos\frac{\beta}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{S(S-b)}{ac}}, \quad \cos\frac{\gamma}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{S(S-c)}{ab}}$$

Sines of half angles:

$$\sin\frac{\alpha}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-b)(S-c)}{bc}}, \quad \sin\frac{\beta}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-a)(S-c)}{ac}}, \quad \sin\frac{\gamma}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-a)(S-b)}{ab}}$$

Tangents of half angles:

$$\tan\frac{\alpha}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-b)(S-c)}{S(S-a)}}, \quad \tan\frac{\beta}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-a)(S-c)}{S(S-b)}}, \quad \tan\frac{\gamma}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(S-a)(S-b)}{S(S-c)}}$$

Example

Solve triangle $ABC$ with $a = 5$, $b = 6$, $c = 7$ using half-angle cosines.

$S = \frac{5+6+7}{2} = 9$

$\cos\frac{\alpha}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{9 \times 4}{6 \times 7}} = \sqrt{\frac{36}{42}} = \sqrt{0.857} \approx 0.926$

$\frac{\alpha}{2} \approx 22.18° \Rightarrow \alpha \approx 44.36°$

$\cos\frac{\beta}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{9 \times 3}{5 \times 7}} = \sqrt{0.771} \approx 0.878 \Rightarrow \beta \approx 57.20°$

$\gamma = 180° - 44.36° - 57.20° = 78.44°$

4 Area of a Triangle (Multiple Formulas) ▼

(a) Two sides and included angle (SAS):

$$\Delta = \frac{1}{2}bc\sin\alpha = \frac{1}{2}ac\sin\beta = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin\gamma$$

(b) Two angles and one side (AAS):

$$\Delta = \frac{a^2 \sin\beta\sin\gamma}{2\sin\alpha} = \frac{b^2 \sin\alpha\sin\gamma}{2\sin\beta} = \frac{c^2 \sin\alpha\sin\beta}{2\sin\gamma}$$

(c) Three sides (Heron's Formula):

$$\Delta = \sqrt{S(S-a)(S-b)(S-c)} \quad\text{where } S = \frac{a+b+c}{2}$$

Example — Heron's Formula

Find the area of a triangle with sides 16, 18, 20.

$S = \frac{16+18+20}{2} = 27$

$\Delta = \sqrt{27(27-16)(27-18)(27-20)} = \sqrt{27 \times 11 \times 9 \times 7} = \sqrt{18711} \approx 136.79$ sq. units

Example — SAS Formula

Find area of triangle $DEF$ if $DE = 10$, $EF = 8$, $\angle E = 30°$.

$\Delta = \frac{1}{2}(10)(8)\sin 30° = 40 \times 0.5 = 20$ sq. units

5 Circumradius (R) of a Triangle ▼
Circumcircle: The circle passing through all three vertices of a triangle. Its centre is the intersection of perpendicular bisectors of the sides.

The circumradius $R$ can be found using:

$$R = \frac{a}{2\sin\alpha} = \frac{b}{2\sin\beta} = \frac{c}{2\sin\gamma}$$

Or in terms of sides and area:

$$R = \frac{abc}{4\Delta}$$

where $\Delta$ is the area of the triangle.

Example

Find $R$ when $a = 8$, $b = 9$, $c = 11$.

$S = 14$, $\Delta = \sqrt{14 \times 6 \times 5 \times 3} = \sqrt{1260} \approx 35.5$

$R = \frac{8 \times 9 \times 11}{4 \times 35.5} = \frac{792}{142} \approx 5.58$

6 Inradius (r) and E-Radii of a Triangle ▼
Incircle: The largest circle that fits inside a triangle, touching all three sides. Its centre is the intersection of angle bisectors.

The inradius $r$:

$$r = \frac{\Delta}{S}$$

where $\Delta$ is the area and $S$ is the semi-perimeter.

Escribed circle (e-circle): A circle touching one side externally and extensions of the other two sides. The e-radii are:

$$r_1 = \frac{\Delta}{S-a}, \quad r_2 = \frac{\Delta}{S-b}, \quad r_3 = \frac{\Delta}{S-c}$$

Example

Find $r$, $R$, and $r_2$ when $a = 8$, $b = 9$, $c = 11$.

$S = 14$, $\Delta \approx 35.5$

$r = \frac{35.5}{14} \approx 2.54$

$R = \frac{792}{142} \approx 5.58$

$r_2 = \frac{35.5}{14 - 9} = \frac{35.5}{5} = 7.1$

Useful identity: $r \cdot r_1 \cdot r_2 \cdot r_3 = \Delta^2$

Question 1 of 8
In right triangle $ABC$ with $a = 2$ cm and $c = 4$ cm (hypotenuse), what is $\alpha$?
A $30°$
B $45°$
C $60°$
D $120°$
Correct! $\sin\alpha = \frac{a}{c} = \frac{2}{4} = 0.5$, so $\alpha = 30°$.
$\sin\alpha = \frac{2}{4} = 0.5 \Rightarrow \alpha = 30°$.
Question 2 of 8
The area of an equilateral triangle with side $a$ is:
A $\frac{a^2}{2}$
B $\frac{a^2}{4}$
C $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2$
D $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}a^2$
Correct! $\Delta = \frac{1}{2}a \cdot a \cdot \sin 60° = \frac{a^2}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2$.
Use the SAS formula with the 60-degree angle: $\Delta = \frac{1}{2}a^2\sin 60° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2$.
Question 3 of 8
Area of triangle $ABC$ with $a = 20$, $b = 30$, $\gamma = 90°$ is:
A 0
B 30
C 300
D 600
Correct! $\Delta = \frac{1}{2}(20)(30)\sin 90° = \frac{1}{2}(600)(1) = 300$.
$\Delta = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin\gamma = \frac{1}{2}(20)(30)(1) = 300$.
Question 4 of 8
For an equilateral triangle, $r : r_1 : R$ equals:
A $1 : 2 : 3$
B $3 : 2 : 1$
C $1 : 1 : 2$
D $1 : 3 : 2$
Correct! For an equilateral triangle, $r : r_1 : R = 1 : 1 : 2$.
For an equilateral triangle with side $a$: $r = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{3}}$, $r_1 = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{3}}$, $R = \frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}$, giving ratio $1:1:2$.
Question 5 of 8
The radius of the circumcircle for a triangle with sides 6, 8, 10 is:
A $3$
B $4$
C $5$
D $6$
Correct! Since $6^2 + 8^2 = 10^2$, it's a right triangle with hypotenuse 10. $R = \frac{c}{2} = \frac{10}{2} = 5$.
This is a right triangle ($6^2+8^2=10^2$). The circumradius of a right triangle equals half the hypotenuse: $R = 5$.
Question 6 of 8
The shadow of a 5.6 ft tall man makes an angle of elevation of 45 degrees with the sun. The length of the shadow is:
A 2.8 ft
B 5.6 ft
C 8.4 ft
D 11.2 ft
Correct! $\tan 45° = \frac{5.6}{\text{shadow}}$. Since $\tan 45° = 1$, shadow $= 5.6$ ft.
$\tan 45° = 1 = \frac{5.6}{\text{shadow}}$, so shadow $= 5.6$ ft.
Question 7 of 8
If $a = 5$, $b = 10$, $c = 20$ are given as sides of a triangle, then:
A Triangle is not possible
B Angle $\alpha$ is acute
C Angle $\alpha$ is obtuse
D Angle $\alpha = 0°$
Correct! $5 + 10 = 15 < 20$. The triangle inequality fails, so no triangle exists.
Check the triangle inequality: $5 + 10 = 15 < 20$. The sum of two sides must exceed the third. No triangle is possible.
Question 8 of 8
If in a triangle $a = 10$, $b = 15$, $\alpha = 32°$, then $\beta$ is approximately:
A $42.5°$
B $52.7°$
C $46.5°$
D $62.8°$
Correct! By Law of Sines: $\sin\beta = \frac{15 \sin 32°}{10} = \frac{15 \times 0.5299}{10} \approx 0.7949$, so $\beta \approx 52.7°$.
$\frac{\sin\beta}{15} = \frac{\sin 32°}{10} \Rightarrow \sin\beta = 0.7949 \Rightarrow \beta \approx 52.7°$.

Boss Problem 1: The Fire Tower

Fire towers A and B are 10 miles apart. Rangers at tower A spot a fire at 42 degrees, and rangers at tower B spot the same fire at 64 degrees. How far is the fire from tower A?

Two sides of a triangular plot have lengths 400 m and 600 m. The angle between them is 45 degrees. Find both the perimeter and area of the plot.

Hint: Use Law of Cosines to find the third side, then the SAS area formula for the area.

Boss Problem 3: Circles in Triangles

Hint: Find $S$ and $\Delta$ first using Heron's formula. Then $r = \frac{\Delta}{S}$ and $R = \frac{abc}{4\Delta}$.
